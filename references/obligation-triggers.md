# Obligation Trigger Words Reference

A comprehensive list of words and phrases that signal regulatory obligations. Use this reference when scanning a regulatory document to make sure you catch every type of obligation, not just the obvious "shall" and "must".

## Mandatory Obligations

These words create binding requirements that must be complied with:

- **shall** - The most common mandatory word in legislation and standards
- **must** - Same force as "shall", more common in modern plain-language drafting
- **will** - Mandatory in many jurisdictions (especially US federal), though sometimes used for future tense
- **is required to** / **are required to**
- **is obligated to** / **has an obligation to**
- **is to** - Common in UK and Australian legislation (e.g., "The licensee is to provide...")
- **ensure that** / **must ensure** - Creates a delegation obligation (you must make sure it happens, even if someone else does the work)
- **is responsible for** - Assigns accountability
- **is liable for** / **is liable to**
- **take reasonable steps to** / **take all reasonable steps**
- **is duty-bound to** / **has a duty to**

## Prohibitions

These words create things that must not be done:

- **must not** / **shall not**
- **is prohibited from**
- **is not permitted to**
- **is not to** - Common in Australian legislation
- **is precluded from**
- **may not** - Note: in some contexts "may not" means "might not" (uncertainty), but in regulatory text it almost always means "is not allowed to"
- **unless approved by** / **except with the consent of** - These create conditional prohibitions (prohibited unless a specific approval is obtained)
- **no person shall** / **a person must not**

## Qualified Mandatory

These words create obligations that have a built-in reasonableness test. They are still mandatory but the standard of compliance is not absolute:

- **must have regard to** / **shall have regard to** - Must genuinely consider, but not necessarily follow
- **must consult with** / **shall consult** - Must engage in consultation, but not necessarily agree
- **must consider** / **shall take into account**
- **to the extent practicable** / **to the extent reasonably practicable**
- **so far as is reasonably practicable (SFAIRP)** - Critical in workplace health and safety law. Creates a mandatory obligation but allows a cost-benefit assessment
- **take all steps that are reasonably necessary**
- **use best endeavours** / **use reasonable endeavours** - "Best endeavours" is close to absolute, "reasonable endeavours" allows more flexibility
- **as far as possible** / **where possible**

When you encounter these, extract them as MUST DO but note the qualification in the Plain English Summary and Implementation Notes. The qualification is important because it affects how compliance is assessed.

## Permissive

These words grant permission or discretion. They don't create obligations on the party they apply to, but they often create obligations on other parties:

- **may** / **can** / **is permitted to**
- **is entitled to** - Grants a right to one party, which usually creates an obligation on another party to provide or allow it
- **has the right to** - Same as "is entitled to"
- **may request** - When paired with an obligation to respond (e.g., "a customer may request X and the retailer must provide it within Y days"), both the right and the response obligation should be extracted
- **at the discretion of** / **as determined by**
- **is authorised to**

Important: When a regulation says "customers are entitled to X", always check whether this creates a corresponding obligation on the service provider. It almost always does, even if the obligation isn't stated explicitly. Extract both the entitlement (as MAY DO or ENTITLEMENT) and the implied provider obligation (as DERIVED, MUST DO).

## Conditional Triggers

These words signal that an obligation only applies in specific circumstances. Extract the condition AND the obligation as separate elements of the requirement:

- **if** / **where** / **when** / **whenever**
- **unless** - The obligation applies EXCEPT when the condition is met
- **provided that** / **on the condition that**
- **subject to** - The obligation applies but is limited by another provision
- **except where** / **except in the case of**
- **in the event that** / **in the event of**
- **upon** / **on receipt of** / **following**

## Reporting and Notification

These words signal obligations to provide information to regulators, customers, or other parties:

- **report** / **submit a report**
- **notify** / **give notice** / **provide notice**
- **inform** / **advise**
- **disclose** / **make available**
- **submit** / **lodge** / **furnish** / **file**
- **provide** (when used with information, data, or documents)
- **publish** / **make publicly available**

## Timeline Triggers

These words signal deadlines or timeframes that are themselves compliance requirements:

- **by [date]** / **no later than [date]**
- **within [period]** - e.g., "within 5 business days"
- **before** / **prior to**
- **as soon as practicable** / **as soon as reasonably practicable** - Not a fixed deadline but still a timeline obligation
- **without delay** / **without undue delay** (GDPR uses this extensively)
- **within a reasonable time**
- **not less than [period] before** - Minimum notice periods
- **at least [period] before**
- **annually** / **quarterly** / **monthly** - Regular reporting cycles

## Jurisdictional Notes

Different jurisdictions have different drafting conventions:

**UK and Australian legislation:**
- Tends to use "must" in modern drafting, "shall" in older Acts
- "Is to" is common and is mandatory (e.g., "The authority is to maintain a register")
- "May" is always permissive, never mandatory
- Regulations often use "A person must not..." rather than "No person shall..."

**US federal (CFR and USC):**
- Uses "shall" extensively, though some agencies have moved to "must"
- "Will" is often mandatory in CFR (not just future tense)
- "No person may..." means prohibition (different from general English usage of "may")
- Preambles in the Federal Register explain intent but are not binding

**EU directives and regulations:**
- "Member States shall ensure..." means the obligation is on governments to transpose or implement
- "The controller shall..." is a direct obligation on organisations
- "Without prejudice to..." means this provision doesn't override another one
- Recitals provide context but are not binding requirements (though courts use them for interpretation)

**ISO and international standards:**
- "Shall" is the only mandatory word per ISO Directives Part 2
- "Should" is a recommendation, not a requirement
- "May" is permission
- "Can" is capability (not permission)
- These distinctions are formally defined and consistent across all ISO standards
